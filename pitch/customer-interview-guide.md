# Customer Interview Guide

This guide is designed for early market validation.

The goal is not to ask whether people "like" the idea. The goal is to test whether property operators, developers, apartments, parks, or campuses have real pain, data access, budget, and willingness to operate the product.

## Target Interviewees

Prioritize:

- property company executives;
- community property managers;
- real estate developer product or brand teams;
- high-end apartment operators;
- park or campus operation teams;
- smart city / digital twin solution buyers;
- property SaaS or access-control system providers.

Avoid relying only on casual resident feedback at this stage.

Residents are important, but the first market question is whether the B-side buyer will pay and support deployment.

## Interview Goal

Validate five things:

```text
1. Is the pain real?
2. Is there budget?
3. Can they provide spatial data?
4. Can they operate the backend?
5. Would they pilot or pay?
```

## Opening Script

Short version:

> We are building Community Spirit, an AI-native community digital twin prototype. It helps residents navigate daily tasks, helps property teams operate services and activities spatially, and prepares semantic maps for future service robots. We are not selling yet. We are interviewing potential users and buyers to validate the first pilot scenario.

Chinese version:

> 我们在做一个叫社区精灵的 AI 原生社区数字孪生原型。它希望把真实社区变成可导航、可办事、可活动、可运营、未来可连接服务机器人的数字空间。现在不是正式销售阶段，主要想访谈真实客户，验证第一个样板场景是否值得做。

## Section 1: Current Workflow

Ask:

1. How do new residents currently become familiar with the community?
2. How do visitors find the right gate, building, unit entrance, or parking entrance?
3. How do residents report repair issues with exact locations?
4. How do you publish and manage community activities?
5. How do residents find package lockers, property centers, clubhouses, playgrounds, or other facilities?
6. How do property teams inspect fire passages, garage entrances, waste points, and public facilities?

Listen for:

- repeated manual explanations;
- chat group overload;
- complaints about unclear locations;
- activity management friction;
- staff relying on personal experience instead of structured data.

## Section 2: Pain and Cost

Ask:

1. Which of these problems creates the most workload?
2. Which problem creates the most resident complaints?
3. Which problem affects brand or delivery experience?
4. Which problem would you pay to reduce?
5. Do you have measurable data, such as visitor guidance questions, repair response time, activity signup rates, or complaint volume?

Strong signals:

- "This happens every week."
- "Our front desk spends time explaining this."
- "New owners often complain."
- "Activity signup is hard to manage."
- "Repair location descriptions are often unclear."

Weak signals:

- "It sounds interesting."
- "Young people may like it."
- "Maybe useful in the future."
- "We can try it for free."

## Section 3: Data Availability

Ask:

1. Do you have a site plan, CAD, BIM, floor plan, garage map, or facility list?
2. Who owns these files?
3. Can these files be shared with a vendor under agreement?
4. Do you have a list of gates, buildings, facilities, activity spaces, package lockers, waste points, and emergency passages?
5. How often do these locations change?
6. Are there restrictions on mapping internal community spaces?

Red flags:

- no access to drawings or facility lists;
- unclear data owner;
- security concerns with no approval path;
- property team unwilling to verify points or routes.

## Section 4: Budget and Buyer

Ask:

1. Have you purchased smart community, digital twin, property SaaS, or display systems before?
2. Which department owns this budget?
3. Who signs off on a pilot?
4. Would this be considered brand display, property digitalization, resident service, or smart city work?
5. Do you prefer project deployment fee, annual service fee, or bundled operation service?
6. What price range would be realistic for a pilot?

Strong signals:

- names the decision maker;
- explains budget category;
- compares to past purchases;
- asks for proposal or quote;
- asks about pilot scope and timeline.

## Section 5: MVP Prioritization

Ask:

If the first pilot only includes five modules, which would you choose?

Options:

- 3D / 2.5D community map;
- visitor route sharing;
- package locker and facility navigation;
- repair location reporting;
- activity signup and check-in;
- property dashboard;
- AI community assistant;
- robot patrol route export;
- light neighbor activity channel.

Force ranking:

> Please rank the top three. If we only deliver one in the first month, which one should it be?

## Section 6: Operations

Ask:

1. Who would maintain POIs and activities?
2. Who would review repair reports and user-generated content?
3. Do you already have a property app or mini program?
4. Would this need to integrate with access control, parking, repair, or activity systems?
5. How often could property staff update the backend?

Critical question:

> If the system requires nobody on your side to maintain it, it will become outdated. Who can own it operationally?

## Section 7: Pilot Willingness

Ask:

1. Would you be willing to provide a fictionalized or limited data sample for a demo?
2. Would you be willing to select 10-20 POIs for a pilot?
3. Would you be willing to test visitor guidance, repair reporting, or activity signup with a small user group?
4. Would you be willing to pay for a pilot if the scope and price are controlled?
5. What would need to be true for you to approve a pilot?

Strongest signal:

- a specific community is named;
- a next meeting is scheduled;
- they agree to provide data;
- they ask for a written pilot proposal;
- they discuss price, legal, or deployment timeline.

## Post-interview Scoring

Score each interview from 1 to 5.

### Pain Score

1 = vague interest only
3 = real pain but not urgent
5 = frequent pain with clear operational cost

### Data Access Score

1 = no data access
3 = some data exists but approval unclear
5 = data owner can provide maps, POIs, and verification support

### Budget Score

1 = no budget
3 = possible budget but unclear buyer
5 = clear budget owner and purchase path

### Operation Score

1 = nobody can maintain it
3 = possible part-time operator
5 = clear property staff owner

### Pilot Score

1 = no pilot interest
3 = free demo interest
5 = paid or permission-based pilot interest

## Qualification Rule

A strong pilot candidate should score:

```text
Pain >= 4
Data Access >= 4
Budget >= 3
Operation >= 3
Pilot >= 4
```

If a customer likes the idea but scores low on data access and operation, do not start a real pilot there.
